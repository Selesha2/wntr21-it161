Accessibility

Accessibility is a big part of designing a website and is what allows everyone to have equal access to your site. Those with disabilities tend to avoid using the web due to the many barriers they face every time they try so it’s your job to take those barriers down when first starting your web design. You’ll have a ton of people from all walks of life that will need specific needs met in order to use your site and have a good experience along the way. For example, blind people need to have some way of knowing what your site contains so you as a web designer are going to incorporate in a screen reader such as Microsoft Narrator or VoiceOver. “A screen reader translates Web pages into plain text. It then scans through the entire page (in its plain text format) and reads it aloud line by line, one element at a time, progressing down the page in a linear fashion.” (Vorm, 2019) This is why it helps to use the em tag instead of the "i" tag when formatting your content because the screen readers will emphasize the text when reading aloud instead of just visually italicizing it. This allows for those listening to clearly understand the point you are getting across. The visual content you add into your site such as images will also be read aloud so it is always good to have a description in the images alt-tag or else if it is left empty, those listening will miss out on that part of the website. That is why accessibility is so important, so nobody gets left out. A good web designer will take into consideration all the different types of disabilities and what they need in order to have a successful experience. Even if it means extra steps when creating their site. “Web auditory, cognitive-motor, neurological, speech and visual” (Popescu, 2020) disabilities all deserve a positive experience on the web and it’s your job to help them get there.
